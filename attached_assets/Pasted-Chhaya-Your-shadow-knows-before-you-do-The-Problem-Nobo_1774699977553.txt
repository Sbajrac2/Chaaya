What Chhaya Does Differently
Most apps ask: "How do you feel today?"

That question is the problem. When someone is deteriorating, their ability to accurately self-assess their emotional state deteriorates with them. They underreport. They mask. They say fine.

Chhaya doesn't ask how you feel. It watches what you do.

Five behaviorally grounded questions. 60 seconds. Each one chosen for a specific scientific reason.

The Science Behind Every Question
1. "Did you attend class today?" Signal: Academic behavioral withdrawal

Academic disengagement is one of the earliest and most measurable leading indicators of psychological distress in college students — preceding grade drops by 2–3 weeks and counseling self-referral by 4–6 weeks.

Source: Journal of American College Health (2019); Eisenberg et al., "Mental Health and Academic Success in College" (2009)

The key insight: students stop attending before they stop submitting. Attendance is the canary.

2. "What time did you wake up?" Signal: Circadian rhythm disruption

The human circadian system is regulated by the suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN) in the hypothalamus — a biological clock sensitive to light and routine. When wake time drifts by more than 90 minutes from a person's baseline, it indicates circadian disruption — which directly impairs the prefrontal cortex's ability to regulate emotion, make decisions, and sustain motivation.

This is called social jet lag — and college students experience it chronically. A wake time shift of 2+ hours sustained over 5+ days is a measurable neurobiological signal, not just a lifestyle observation.

Source: Roenneberg et al., "Social Jetlag and Obesity" (2012); Walker, "Why We Sleep" (2017); American Academy of Sleep Medicine

3. "Did you leave your room today?" Signal: Voluntary behavioral withdrawal / isolation

Voluntary isolation is one of the most robust behavioral predictors of depression onset in young adults. The mechanism is neurobiological: humans are wired for co-regulation — our nervous systems literally calm down in the presence of safe others. When a student stops leaving their room, they lose access to this co-regulation, which accelerates dysregulation.

Critically: isolation and depression create a feedback loop. Low mood causes isolation. Isolation deepens low mood. Tracking this behavior catches the loop early — before the student has language for what they're experiencing.

Source: Cacioppo & Hawkley, "Perceived Social Isolation and Cognition" (2009, Trends in Cognitive Sciences); APA Div. 12 depression behavioral markers

4. "Did you eat a proper meal today?" Signal: Self-neglect / allostatic load

Irregular eating is both a symptom and an amplifier of psychological stress. The gut-brain axis — the bidirectional communication between the enteric nervous system and the central nervous system via the vagus nerve — means that nutritional disruption directly affects mood, anxiety, and cognitive function through serotonin pathways (90% of serotonin is produced in the gut).

Beyond neurochemistry, skipping meals is a behavioral marker of what psychologists call self-neglect — the unconscious deprioritization of one's own basic needs. This behavioral shift often appears before conscious emotional awareness of distress.

Source: Cryan & Dinan, "Mind-altering microorganisms: the gut microbiome" (2012, Nature Reviews Neuroscience); Stunkard & Allison, eating behavior and psychological distress research

5. "How close were you to your real self today?" (1–5) Signal: Emotional labor / performance tax / masking cost

This question measures what psychologists call emotional dissonance — the gap between felt emotion and displayed emotion. Every hour spent performing a version of yourself that doesn't match your internal state costs measurable cognitive and emotional resources.

This is especially critical for college students because:

High-functioning students mask more effectively and are therefore invisible to existing support systems
The students with the widest performance gap are often the last to self-refer for help
Sustained high masking is one of the strongest predictors of eventual burnout
Tracking this gap over time catches the students that every other system misses.

Source: Hochschild, "The Managed Heart" (1983); Grandey, "Emotional regulation in the workplace" (2000, Journal of Occupational Health Psychology); Nolen-Hoeksema depression masking research

🧠 Additional Factors We Could Add — Expert Recommendations
As a behavioral and neuropsychological expert, here are the factors with the strongest scientific case for inclusion:

Factor 1 — Response Timing (What Time Did You Submit This Check-in?)

The timestamp of the check-in itself is a passive behavioral signal. A student completing their check-in at 2am vs 8pm is a neurobiological data point — it reflects circadian phase, sleep pressure, and behavioral routine integrity without asking a single extra question.

Science: Circadian rhythm research shows behavioral timing is as diagnostically informative as self-reported sleep duration.

Factor 2 — Physical Contact / Touch Deprivation

"Did you have any physical contact with another person today — a hug, handshake, or even a pat on the back?"

This sounds unusual but has robust neuroscientific backing. Human touch releases oxytocin and activates the vagus nerve — both of which directly regulate stress response. Touch deprivation in college students (especially those living alone or far from family) is a documented but massively undertracked stressor.

Source: Field, "Touch deprivation and exercise deficiency in depressed adolescents" (2005); Hertenstein et al., touch and communication research

Factor 3 — Cognitive Friction "Did you find it hard to start tasks today, even simple ones?"

This measures activation energy — the neurological cost of initiating behavior. Elevated activation energy (procrastination, task paralysis, inability to start) is one of the clearest early markers of prefrontal cortex underperformance caused by sleep deprivation, chronic stress, or early depressive episodes.

Students often experience this before they experience sadness. It's the "I can't make myself do anything" feeling that precedes the "I feel terrible" awareness by days.

Source: Barkley, executive function and self-regulation research; depression and anergia literature

Factor 4 — Sunlight Exposure (Actual, Not Calculated) "Did you spend any time in natural daylight today?"

We currently calculate sunlight hours from weather data — but actual exposure matters more than available light. A student can have 10 hours of sunshine outside and spend all of it in a windowless library. Direct retinal light exposure is what drives serotonin synthesis and suppresses melatonin during the day.

One binary question captures something no API can calculate.

Source: Lewy et al., light therapy and circadian rhythm research; NIH seasonal affective disorder studies

Factor 5 — Substance Use Proxy "Did you rely on caffeine or alcohol to get through today?"

Not asking about addiction — asking about coping mechanism dependency as a behavioral signal. Increased caffeine dependency correlates with sleep debt accumulation. Alcohol use in college students spikes during high-stress periods and is both a symptom and an amplifier of distress.

This question catches a behavioral shift that students rarely volunteer but is highly informative.

Source: NIAAA college drinking research; caffeine and sleep debt literature

Factor 6 — Completion Sense "Did you finish at least one thing you intended to do today?"

This measures behavioral momentum — a concept from behavioral activation therapy (one of the most evidence-backed treatments for depression). The inability to complete even small intended tasks is both a symptom of and contributor to low mood. Tracking this catches the "everything feels unfinished" spiral early.

Source: Behavioral Activation Therapy literature; Martell, Addis & Jacobson (2001)
